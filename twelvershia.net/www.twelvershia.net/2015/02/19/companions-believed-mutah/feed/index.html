Abrogation will sometimes create confusion. This is common in the books of Tafseer and law. Scholars differed as to whether a ruling was abrogated or not. One doesn’t need to understand how Ibn Abbas formulated his opinion to arrive at the conclusion that he was mistaken. We don’t even need to know what led Ibn Juraij to abandon mut’ah after practicing it for a long time. All we know is that he was mistaken and that he repented from his actions.

Another possibility is that Ibn Abbas never heard the Prophet – peace be upon him – declaring mut’ah halal or haram, rather, it was second-hand information that he got from another Sahabi, whoever it may be. Decades later, he is introduced to this alternative opinion, and it is not a common thing for one to assume that his early source was wrong after believing in something for so many years.

As I said, we could keep theorizing about his reasons for a while. Though, in the end, all we will be doing is theorizing.

Also, please don’t refer to mut’ah as zina. Ibn Abbas didn’t call people to zina, nor did the Prophet – peace be upon him – make zina permissible temporarily. Anyone who says otherwise is plain wrong.

Wa alaykum alsalam,

It is hard to say what Ibn Abbas had in mind. The only clear matter is that he did not believe that there is clear-cut proof of Mutah being forbidden. Perhaps the most likely scenario is that he heard the Prophet – peace be upon him – declaring that it is halal, but never heard him saying that it was haram. He may have assumed that those that said that it was haram misunderstood the Prophet – peace be upon him – . The possibilities are plenty. As Sunnis, it is not our duty to speculate one’s reasons for their beliefs. Our duty is to simply follow the opinion that has the strongest evidence.
